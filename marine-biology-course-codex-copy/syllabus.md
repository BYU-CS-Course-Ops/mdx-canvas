# Introduction to Marine Biology

## Course Format

- Duration: 2 weeks, 10 instructional days
- Delivery: Lecture, lab, and field observation
- Prerequisites: None

## Course Description

This intensive course introduces students to the physical, chemical, and biological systems that shape marine life.
Across two weeks, students examine ocean structure, seawater chemistry, planktonic food webs, coral reefs, estuaries,
the deep sea, marine mammals, elasmobranchs, sea turtles, and the human pressures affecting ocean ecosystems.

Each day combines core content with applied observation, discussion, or analysis so that students connect marine
biology concepts to real ecological questions.

## Learning Outcomes

By the end of the course, students should be able to:

1. Explain major physical and chemical properties of the ocean.
2. Compare major marine ecosystems and the organisms adapted to them.
3. Describe the ecological roles of plankton, benthic organisms, and marine megafauna.
4. Interpret marine conservation issues using biological evidence.
5. Communicate marine science findings through written, visual, and collaborative work.

## Course Schedule

### Week 1

| Day | Topic | Focus |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | Welcome to the Sea | Course orientation, history of marine biology, why the ocean matters |
| 2 | Ocean Geography | Ocean basins, tectonics, bathymetry, seafloor mapping |
| 3 | Seawater Chemistry | Salinity, density, dissolved gases, nutrient cycling, acidification |
| 4 | Phytoplankton and Primary Production | Photosynthesis, productivity, chlorophyll, blooms, dead zones |
| 5 | Zooplankton and Food Webs | Trophic structure, plankton diversity, carbon transfer |

### Week 2

| Day | Topic | Focus |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 6 | Coral Reefs | Coral biology, reef zonation, bleaching, restoration |
| 7 | Estuaries and Mangroves | Brackish systems, nursery habitat, tidal influence, coastal protection |
| 8 | The Deep Sea | Hydrothermal vents, pressure adaptations, bioluminescence |
| 9 | Marine Mammals | Cetaceans, pinnipeds, otters, migration, social behavior |
| 10 | Sharks, Rays, and Sea Turtles | Sensory biology, navigation, bycatch, conservation policy |

## Assessment Breakdown

| Component | Weight | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Daily Participation | 15% | Preparedness, discussion, field and lab engagement |
| Lab Notebook | 25% | Ongoing observations, data, sketches, and reflections |
| Midweek Quiz | 15% | Covers Units 1 and 2 |
| Group Presentation | 20% | Team-based ecosystem case study |
| Final Reflection Essay | 25% | 1,500-word synthesis on a marine issue |

## Participation Expectations

- Arrive prepared to discuss the day’s topic.
- Contribute to lab, observation, and collaborative activities.
- Keep your lab notebook current after each session.
- Use evidence from readings, notes, and observations when making claims.

## Late Work

Because this is an intensive course, major assignments should be submitted on schedule. If an issue affects your work,
contact the instructor as early as possible so an alternate plan can be documented.

## Academic Practice

Students are expected to complete their own written work, contribute meaningfully to group tasks, and cite any outside
sources or media used in presentations and the final essay.
